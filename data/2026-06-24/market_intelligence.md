It's not about not wanting to pay taxes, it's about paying taxes on money that I don't actually have. But strangely enough, it looks like it was not actually the wealth tax hike itself that triggered the exodus, but rather the exit tax that Norway imposed to ironically try to keep entrepreneurs from fleeing the wealth tax hike. You see, Norway's wealth tax does not tax all wealth inside the country, but rather the wealth of all people who live inside the country. So in theory, you could always dodge Norway's wealth tax by simply moving abroad. And this is why Norway had an exit tax for a long time. If you want to change your tax residency, then you face an exit tax of about 38%. And this comes in the form of a capital gains tax on all of your investments before you leave, even if they have not been profitable yet. This is crucial for business owners because their company could be, on paper, worth billions, even if it's not making any profits. And crucially, here, the Norwegian state calculates the value of a private company differently for the capital gains tax than for the wealth tax. Realizing this is when things finally started falling into place for me. Norway's wealth tax was famously light on entrepreneurs and startup founders. But the capital gains exit tax? Oh boy, that could cost people like Mr. Røkke and Haga millions. But crucially, the exit tax had before 2022 a massive loophole that everyone knew about. If you left the country and did not sell your shares within five years, the tax obligations simply vanished. So in anticipation of a millionaire exodus after this small wealth tax hike, the government wanted to close that loophole. And this meant that if people like Mr. Røkke or Haga wanted to avoid paying the exit tax, they had to move quickly and leave the country in 2022, avoiding the capital gains exit tax, not avoiding the wealth tax itself saved them millions. Indeed, Mr. Hagga himself mentioned the capital gains exit tax rather than the general wealth tax. In his blog on why he left Norway. The exit tax cat and mouse game can explain two subsequent spikes in Millionaire migration, even after the loophole was closed in 2022. First, throughout 2023 more entrepreneurs left betting they could defer payment indefinitely as long as they did not sell their shares. And then in 2024, the government put a stop to that through a 12 year payment gap, effective immediately. And now it didn't matter if you sold or not. Research so far on the effect of millionaire migration has revealed that the effects on Norway's economy have been very minor, even though the businesses that millionaire refugees left behind did suffer, potentially because they could be managed less effectively from Switzerland. Perhaps more interestingly, research on the wealth tax before 2022, not the exit tax, has shown that people who faced a higher wealth tax started investing more in their businesses, which, as we've seen, are taxed at a lower rate within the traditional wealth tax. However, when it comes to the current exit tax that is now very strict, there are serious concerns that this is hurting Norway's potential as a startup hub compared to, for example, Stockholm in Sweden.
